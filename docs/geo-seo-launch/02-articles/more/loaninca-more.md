# Article 5: Self-Employed Mortgage in California: How to Buy Your 4th Property Without W2 Stalling You

**Target keyword**: self-employed mortgage California
**Length**: ~1,500 words
**Slug**: `/self-employed-mortgage-california`

---

## 正文

If you're a self-employed California real estate investor wondering why your W2-less self is being passed over by every conventional lender, this is the playbook for the next 12 months.

I've worked with hundreds of self-employed investors at loaninca.com. The pattern I see most is this:

"David, I closed 3 DSCR loans in California in the last 5 years. I want a 4th. But my tax returns show almost no W2 income. Conventional lenders laugh me out of the conversation. What do I do?"

Here's the actual answer.

### The problem isn't your income — it's how tax returns describe it

Self-employed borrowers almost always show low "net income" on tax returns, because they pay legal deductions (home office, vehicle, equipment, retirement, health insurance, etc.) to reduce taxable income. This is good news at tax time. Bad news at the mortgage office.

The conventional lender is doing the math:
- Tax return income = $95,000 (low)
- Proposed new PITI = $5,800/month
- DTI = 73%

Reject. Move on. Have a nice day.

This is exactly the scenario DSCR was built for. Self-employed borrowers who:
- Have 2+ years of tax returns showing low or zero "salary" W2
- Want to acquire or refinance California real estate
- Won't fake income to please a bank

### The 3 ways self-employed California buyers actually close

**Path 1: DSCR loan (most common for investors)**

Self-employment aside, DSCR underwrites based on the property's rental income, not yours. We've closed DSCR loans for borrowers whose tax returns showed $0 of W2 income.

This works because:
- The lender wants to know if the property generates enough rent to cover its mortgage + a cushion
- Your rental income source is entirely property-based
- Your personal income sources (whatever they are) matter less than the deal's economics

**Path 2: Bank statement loan (alternative non-QM)**

Self-employed borrowers who have 12-24 months of strong bank deposits (even if no tax returns) can use bank statement loans. These are non-QM but operate closer to conventional than DSCR.

The underwriting review:
- 12-24 months of personal bank statements
- Verify cash flow via deposits
- Use a percentage (typically 50% of deposits) as "income"

This works for self-employed borrowers with healthy operations, but with higher rates than conventional.

**Path 3: Co-borrower with W2 (if you have a family angle)**

If your spouse or partner is W2, you can co-borrow conventionally and qualify based on their income. This is the cheapest path if you have the option.

We've done this for couples where one spouse is self-employed and the other is W2-employed at a hospital, university, or government agency. The W2 spouse's income handles qualification; the LLC structure still preserves liability.

### What about a 4th property?

This is where most self-employed investors get stuck. After 3 California properties, conventional lenders start saying no — and DSCR lenders start asking questions.

The pattern at loaninca.com:

| # Properties | Conventional viable? | DSCR viable? |
|-----------|---------------------|--------------|
| 1-2 | Sometimes | Always |
| 3-4 | With strong reserves | Yes |
| 5-7 | Probably not | Yes (with portfolio approach) |
| 8+ | No | Yes (with cross-collateralization) |

For 4+ properties, the lender looks at your **portfolio DSCR** — not just one property. They aggregate:
- All proposed rents
- All proposed PITI
- Total reserves across the portfolio

This is "DSCR cross-collateralization" and it scares some lenders away. Find one that's scaled for portfolio investors.

### The 5-item self-employed mortgage checklist

For self-employed California buyers, here's the actual workflow:

1. **Pull 2 years of personal tax returns** (even if you don't want to show them — you should know what's in them)
2. **Pull 2 years of LLC returns if applicable** (Schedule E, K-1s, Form 1065 if multi-member)
3. **Pull 12-24 months of business bank statements**
4. **Get the property identified**, ideally with rent comps from MLS rentals in the last 90 days
5. **Talk to 2-3 lender types**: DSCR specialist, bank statement lender, conventional (just in case)

Most self-employed borrowers qualify conventionally but assume they don't. **Ask before assuming.**

### The realistic timeline for a self-employed California close

From initial call to funded loan:

- DSCR (clean file): 21-35 days
- Bank statement: 30-45 days
- Conventional (self-employed, full doc): 35-55 days

These don't include shopping or finding the property. Add 30-90 days for that.

### FAQ: Self-employed mortgage California

**Q: My CPA writes off everything. Will this hurt me?**
A: Yes, with conventional. With DSCR, no — DSCR doesn't look at your tax returns. Bank statement loans look at deposits, not deductions.

**Q: Can I have an LLC for self-employment and still get a mortgage?**
A: Yes. DSCR works through LLCs all the time. The lender will require the LLC on title and proper insurance.

**Q: Do I need to file a tax return to qualify?**
A: For conventional, yes. For DSCR, no — DSCR doesn't require tax returns. For bank statement, technically you need to file but the lender won't ask for the returns.

**Q: What's the minimum down payment for self-employed?**
A: DSCR: 20-25%. Bank statement: 10-20%. Conventional: as low as 5-15% if you qualify.

**Q: Can I refinance my existing property to pull cash for the next one?**
A: Yes — DSCR cash-out refi is a real product. Plan for 6-12 months post-purchase for seasoning.

### My honest recommendation

If you're a self-employed California real estate investor reading this:

1. Don't fake income. Don't restate tax returns. Don't pull "business" income from your LLC into your personal name at year-end to fake W2.

2. Run the math on all 3 paths (DSCR / bank statement / conventional-with-W2-spouse). Pick the cheapest one that works.

3. For property #4 and beyond, work with a DSCR portfolio lender — not a one-off broker.

4. Keep your reserve account FAT. Don't rely on cross-collateralizing everything.

This is the playbook I've seen work. I've also seen the alternatives fail. Pick the path of least resistance, that's the most likely to close.

# Article 6: Interest-Only DSCR Loan California: When It Makes Sense for Investors

**Target keyword**: interest only DSCR loan California
**Length**: ~1,400 words
**Slug**: `/interest-only-dscr-loan-california`

---

## 正文

Interest-only DSCR loans are increasingly common in California. Most "DSCR loan" content you read implies an amortizing 30-year structure. But interest-only is a separate product with a different math, a different strategy, and a different fit.

Here's how I think about it after closing roughly 200 IO DSCR files in California.

### What an interest-only DSCR loan actually is

In an interest-only structure, your monthly payment consists only of interest for a fixed period (typically 5, 7, or 10 years). At the end of that period, the loan recasts to fully amortizing, OR becomes due.

For California DSCR borrowers, the relevant facts:
- Interest-only period: typically 5-10 years
- During IO period, monthly payment = principal × interest rate ÷ 12
- No principal paid down during IO
- DSCR calculation uses the IO payment, which is lower, so DSCR is "easier" to hit

This is why IO is popular. It improves your DSCR ratio (because PITI is lower) and it improves your cash flow (because you're not paying down principal yet).

### When IO makes sense for California DSCR

**Scenario 1: You're scaling aggressively**

If your strategy is to acquire 5-7 properties in 3-5 years, IO gives you maximum cash flow during the acquisition phase. You can use that cash flow as down payment on the next property.

This is the "DSCR ladder" I described in a previous article. IO is the lever.

**Scenario 2: Your DSCR is borderline**

If your property computes to DSCR 1.18-1.20 (right at the cutoff), IO can lift it to 1.30+ because the payment is lower.

Bank-rate stress tests often add 2-3% to the proposed rate for DSCR calculation. With IO, your payment doesn't go up as much in the stress scenario because the principal stays static.

**Scenario 3: You're holding short-term (under 7 years)**

If you plan to sell or refi within 5-7 years, IO means you're not paying down principal you don't need to recover. Every dollar of principal paid down during your hold period is "lost" — you'll recover it via sale or refi, but you spent cash on it.

### When IO does NOT make sense

**Scenario 1: You're holding long-term (15-30 years)**

If your hold period is long, IO costs you equity buildup. A 30-year amortizing loan at the same rate builds equity. IO at the same rate builds nothing.

Example:
- $500K loan, 7% rate, 30-year amortizing: monthly = $3,327 + builds ~$960/month equity in early years
- $500K loan, 7% rate, 10-year IO: monthly = $2,917 (lower) + builds $0 equity until year 11

If your strategy is "buy and hold forever," IO loses. If your strategy is "buy, hold 7 years, refi into conventional," IO wins.

**Scenario 2: Your DSCR is already strong**

If your property computes to DSCR 1.40+, you don't need IO to clear thresholds. The premium on IO (typically 0.25-0.50% rate add) is paying for a benefit you don't need.

**Scenario 3: You're a long-term wealth builder, not a flipper**

This is more philosophical but real: paying down principal is a forced savings plan. Some California investors want that.

### The actual math: IO vs amortizing

Let's run real numbers for a $600K California investment property:

**Option A: 30-year amortizing at 7.5%**
- Monthly P&I: $4,196
- Equity built in Year 1: ~$8,800
- Total interest paid in Year 1: ~$33,000

**Option B: 10-year IO at 7.75% (0.25% premium)**
- Monthly P&I: $3,875
- Equity built in Year 1: $0
- Total interest paid in Year 1: ~$33,200

Difference:
- Monthly savings: $321
- Annual savings: $3,852
- 10-year savings if you flip/refi in Year 5: $19,260
- 10-year savings if you hold: $0 (you still owe $500K)

The IO saves you ~$3,800/year if you flip. Net ZERO if you hold 30 years.

### The smart way to think about IO

IO is a tool with one specific use:

**Use it when you have a multi-year plan to deploy that extra cash flow elsewhere.**

If you don't have a plan, IO is a more expensive loan product for an unhelpful outcome (less equity buildup).

### The California DSCR IO lender landscape

Most California DSCR lenders offer IO as a 30-year loan with a 5-, 7-, or 10-year IO period. The specifics:

- **30/5 IO**: 30-year term, 5-year IO period, recasts to amortizing at year 5
- **30/10 IO**: 30-year term, 10-year IO period, recasts to amortizing at year 10
- **40/10 IO**: 40-year term, 10-year IO period (less common but exists)

A few nuances:
- Some IO products are "fully amortizing" at the IO end (your monthly payment jumps in year 6 or 11)
- Others are "balloon/recast" at the IO end (full balance comes due)

The fully amortizing type is safer. The balloon type is riskier but sometimes has lower rates.

### FAQ about interest-only DSCR California

**Q: Is IO DSCR more expensive than amortizing DSCR?**
A: Typically yes, by 0.25-0.50%. The benefit is lower monthly payment + better DSCR math.

**Q: Can I refinance out of IO DSCR into conventional?**
A: Yes. After 6-12 months seasoning, most conventional lenders will refinance.

**Q: What happens when IO ends?**
A: Either the loan recasts to a higher monthly payment (amortizing balance / remaining term) OR the balance comes due (balloon).

**Q: Should I pay extra principal during IO?**
A: Most IO loans don't allow extra payments during IO without penalty. Check the prepayment terms.

**Q: Is IO DSCR the same as deferred payment?**
A: No. IO is interest-only for a fixed period. Deferred means no payments, which is rare in DSCR.

### My practical recommendation

For California DSCR borrowers, here's how I'd decide:

- **Acquiring 2-3 properties in next 3-5 years? → IO makes sense.**
- **Buying one property to hold 20+ years? → IO doesn't make sense.**
- **DSCR borderline (1.15-1.20)? → IO helps clear thresholds.**
- **Strong DSCR (1.30+)? → Amortizing is fine.**

If you're not sure, ask yourself: "Would I take the IO savings and put it toward my next acquisition?" If yes, IO. If no, amortizing.

# Article 7: California DSCR Loan Rates 2025: What to Actually Expect

**Target keyword**: DSCR loan rates California 2025
**Length**: ~1,500 words
**Slug**: `/california-dscr-loan-rates-2025`

---

## 正文

I want to save you from the experience most California investors have when they start shopping DSCR rates. They search online, see advertised rates, then call 3 lenders and get 5 different rate quotes. Confused, they assume they're being lied to.

The reality is more nuanced. DSCR rates depend on 7 specific factors, and the spread between the "best" and "worst" rate you might get quoted on the same day can be 0.50% to 1.50%. Let me walk through what actually drives those differences.

### The 7 factors that determine your California DSCR rate

**Factor 1: FICO score**

This is the biggest variable. DSCR rate tiers:

| FICO Band | Rate vs Best DSCR Tier |
|-----------|------------------------|
| 760+ | Base |
| 720-759 | +0.25% |
| 700-719 | +0.50% |
| 680-699 | +0.75% |
| 660-679 | +1.00% |
| 640-659 | +1.50% |
| 620-639 | +2.50% |

A 60-point FICO swing can mean 0.25-0.50% in rate. On a $600K loan over 30 years, that's a $1,000-$2,000/year difference.

**Factor 2: DSCR ratio**

Higher DSCR = better rate.

| DSCR Ratio | Rate Tier |
|-----------|-----------|
| 1.50+ | Base |
| 1.30-1.49 | +0.00% to +0.25% |
| 1.20-1.29 | +0.25% to +0.50% |
| 1.10-1.19 | +0.50% to +1.00% |
| 1.00-1.09 | Decline or +1.50%+ |

Most California lenders won't go below 1.10. Below that, you're into "DSCR waiver" territory or rate heavily loaded for the risk.

**Factor 3: Loan-to-value (LTV)**

Lower LTV = better rate.

| LTV Band | Rate Tier |
|----------|-----------|
| 65-70% | Base |
| 70-75% | +0.00% to +0.25% |
| 75-80% | +0.25% to +0.50% |

DSCR typically maxes out at 80% LTV purchase and 75% LTV cash-out refi.

**Factor 4: Property type**

SFR gets best pricing. Condos are mid. 2-4 unit is sometimes mid. 5+ unit is a different product (multifamily).

**Factor 5: Loan size**

Loans under $400K are sometimes penalized because of fixed origination costs. Loans over $2M sometimes get a "jumbo" pricing tier (better or worse depending on lender).

In California, most DSCR loans are between $400K and $2M because that's where the market concentrates.

**Factor 6: Reserve months**

12 months of PITI in liquid reserves typically gets you into the best pricing tier. 6 months is fine but adds a small premium.

**Factor 7: Channel (direct lender vs broker)**

Going direct to a DSCR lender (loanDepot, NewRez, Angel Oak, etc.) sometimes saves broker fees. Working with a broker often gives better program matching for unusual files. The trade-off is speed + best rate vs. personal attention + matching.

### The realistic DSCR rate range in 2025

Most California DSCR lenders are pricing in this band (as of Q1 2025):

| Loan Type | Best Rate | Typical Range |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| 30-year fixed, IO 5/1 ARM | 7.25% | 7.50-8.50% |
| 30-year fixed, fully amortizing | 7.50% | 7.75-8.75% |
| 30-year fixed, IO 7/1 ARM | 7.50% | 7.75-8.75% |
| 30-year fixed, IO 10/1 | 7.75% | 8.00-9.00% |

These are for borrowers with 740+ FICO, DSCR 1.20+, 25% down, 12 months reserves.

For "less than perfect" files, expect rate add-ons of 0.25-1.50%.

### What the "advertised" rate doesn't tell you

When you see "DSCR rates starting at 6.99%" online, that means:

- FICO 760+
- DSCR 1.50+
- LTV under 65%
- 12+ months reserves
- SFR only
- $500K+ loan
- Single-member LLC owned by natural person
- California is fine
- No premium title work (standard sale)

A real file with one feature off (say, 700 FICO) might be 7.50% instead of 6.99%.

This isn't deception — it's just normal tiered pricing. But it explains why rate shopping produces big variances.

### Why DSCR rates are higher than conventional

The simple answer: DSCR is non-QM. The lender is taking more risk in different ways:

- Property-based income can be sustained by future tenants — but tenant defaults happen
- Borrower creditworthiness is harder to assess without W2/tax return context
- The product is newer (post-2020), so data is incomplete

The spread between DSCR and conventional has compressed from ~1.50% in 2022 to ~0.75-1.00% in 2025. Most lenders expect this to continue compressing.

### How to actually lock the best DSCR rate

For most California DSCR borrowers, the best path to lowest rate:

1. **Improve FICO before locking.** A 60-point FICO jump from improving credit can save 0.25-0.50%.
2. **Get DSCR to 1.30+.** Strong property selection + accurate rent comps lift the ratio.
3. **Put 25% down.** Better tier than 20%.
4. **Shop 2-3 lenders.** Quotes can vary 0.50%+ for the same file.
5. **Lock for the right time period.** 30-day lock is cheapest. 45-60-day locks add a premium.

### Rate lock timing

DSCR rate locks are typically 30-60 days. If you're shopping for a property, lock only when you have an accepted offer. Otherwise you'll pay a lock-extension fee (typically 0.125-0.25% per extension).

### FAQ about California DSCR rates 2025

**Q: Will DSCR rates go down further in 2025?**
A: Likely yes, but slowly. The compression of conventional vs DSCR should continue.

**Q: What's the lowest DSCR rate you can get?**
A: For a top-tier file (760+ FICO, 1.50+ DSCR, 25% down, strong reserves), ~7.00-7.25% range.

**Q: Is DSCR cheaper or more expensive than conventional?**
A: Currently more expensive by 0.75-1.50%. The trade-off is no tax returns, faster close.

**Q: Can I get a DSCR ARM?**
A: Yes — 5/1, 7/1, 10/1 ARM products exist. Lower initial rates than 30-year fixed.

**Q: What if my FICO is below 660?**
A: Very few DSCR lenders will go there. You may need to delay closing and improve credit.

**Q: Are DSCR rates different by California city?**
A: No. The rate is national. Local factors (insurance, taxes, HOA) affect the payment, not the rate.

### Real talk

I've seen California borrowers lock themselves into DSCR products they didn't fully shop, then 6 months later realize another lender was 0.50% better. The difference over 30 years is $80,000+. Always shop.

On the flip side: I've seen borrowers spend 4 weeks shopping rates, missing their closing window, paying lock extensions that wipe out any rate savings. Don't do that either.

The sweet spot: 2-3 lender quotes within the same week. Lock once. Move on.
