## 正文

This is the question I get most often. Variations:

- "If I'm in California and my parents pass away in Shanghai, can I do anything from here?"
- "I light incense at the temple maybe twice a year. Does it really matter that I can't go back?"
- "I'd like to commission a prayer for my grandfather, but the only option I see is online. Is that legitimate?"

I'm not a monk. I'm not a theologian. I'm the founder of fopusha.com, an overseas Chinese who built a platform specifically because my grandmother couldn't fly back to her village temple anymore, and because the question I kept getting asked was exactly this one — and the answers I kept finding were either too traditionalist ("you must be there in person, online doesn't count") or too commercial ("yes we offer online service, click here").

I want to try a different answer.

### The technical answer

There are roughly two strands of Buddhism most overseas Chinese are part of: the Mahayana / Chan (Zen) tradition of mainland China and Taiwan, and the Pure Land tradition that overlays everything Chinese Buddhism does, especially around death anniversaries and merit dedication.

In both traditions, the value of a ritual is measured by:
1. **The sincerity of intention** (诚意 chéngyì)
2. **The correctness of the form** (如法 rúfǎ)
3. **The merit generated by the act** (功德 gōngdé)

For most rituals — chanting sutras, dedicating merit to ancestors, lighting incense, giving dāna — all three are achievable from anywhere. The form requires a monk or authorized practitioner. The sincerity comes from the person performing or commissioning the act. The merit arises regardless of physical location.

This is why the mainstream answer in Chinese Buddhism has been, for centuries: where you are matters less than the structure (form + intent) you bring.

### The lived answer

I lost my grandmother in 2023. She was 91. The COVID era had already closed off trips. By 2023, she was frail enough that travel was out of the question. I commissioned her 周年 (annual memorial) from California through a temple in her home village in Guangdong.

I had a monk chant the Earth Store Sutra (地藏经 Dìzàng Jīng). I had name tablets commissioned at the temple's ancestor hall. I had merit dedicated on her behalf during the Ullambana festival. I received photos, video of the chanting, and a sealed certificate with the temple's seal.

Was it the same as being there? No. I would've loved to be there.
Did it actually do anything?

This is where most discussions get stuck. Because what counts as "doing something" depends on what you think prayer does in the first place.

If you believe Buddhist rituals work through spiritual mechanics — direct cosmic response, alignment with cosmic forces — then the question is genuinely moot without empirical verification. Buddhism's traditional answer is that we don't have that kind of verification; we have texts, lineage, and community testimony.

If you believe (and this is more my read) that Buddhist rituals work because they are vehicles for focused intention and filial act within a community — then yes, online is real. The form is the same. The sincerity is the same. The lineage is intact.

### What actually happens when you commission an online Buddhist service

I'm going to walk through what fopusha.com does for a real client case. Names changed for privacy.

**The case:**
A daughter in Seattle. Her mother died in 2024 in Shenzhen, mid-pandemic travel restrictions. She couldn't make it back for the funeral. The father is still alive and elderly. She'd like to commemorate her mother and pray for her father's wellbeing.

**Step 1: Setting the date.**
We work with temples across China and Taiwan. For a major memorial (头七 1st 49-day, 三七、 七七, 49-day cycle completion, 周年 annual), temple calendars matter. We coordinate so the service happens during a temple's actual ceremony schedule — not just when the requester can pay.

**Step 2: Tablet commissioning.**
For major ancestors, name tablets (牌位 páiwèi) are placed in the temple's ancestor hall. These tablets can be:
- 永久牌位 (permanent, name stays in temple permanently)
- 短期牌位 (term, e.g., 1 year, 3 years)
- 节庆牌位 (festival-specific tablets during Lunar New Year, Ullambana, etc.)

The temple I work with most in Guangdong did all three options. For overseas Chinese, 永久牌位 is the most common.

**Step 3: Chanting / sutra recitation.**
Depending on the occasion, the temple conducts:
- 地藏经 (Earth Store Sutra, for deceased)
- 药师经 (Medicine Buddha Sutra, for the living and the ill)
- 心经 / 大悲咒 (Heart Sutra / Great Compassion Mantra, shorter commitments)

**Step 4: Dedication and reporting.**
After the ritual, the family receives:
- Photos of the ceremony
- Video of the chanting (often a portion)
- Sealed certificate from the temple
- Mass card or service card (sometimes a printed record of names and dates)

**Step 5: Anniversary follow-up.**
Optional: we set up annual or 3-year commemoration cycles automatically.

### When online doesn't work

Honest answer: there are cases where physical presence matters and online cannot substitute.

- **The funeral or cremation itself.** Family presence matters for the funeral procession, the urn ceremony, the receiving of condolences.
- **Major 49-day cycle.** Some families and temples strongly prefer in-person or partial in-person participation for the final 49th day.
- **Rituals requiring body or cremated remains.** If the family retained ashes or bones, the temple's physical ritual is the framework.
- **Temple-specific festivals.** Some temples have community-only festivals where only members of the local community participate.

These are usually addressed case-by-case. The conversation is not "online replaces in-person" but rather "online is the path I can commit to, given distance."

### Specific scenarios — practical guidance

**If your parent just passed in China and you're overseas:**

- The first 49 days are crucial. You can commission a 七七 (49-day cycle) of chanting from your home country. Many temples offer this.
- A 牌位 (tablet) should be commissioned within the first 49 days for the principal ancestor.
- Ullambana (July 15 lunar) is the next major ceremony. Plan ahead.

**If you want to honor a deceased ancestor years later:**

- You can commission a retrospective memorial. Temples will add the name to the chanting rolls and sometimes place a tablet.
- Anniversary (周年) cycles can be commissioned yearly.

**If you want to pray for a living parent:**

- 药师经 for healing or longevity is appropriate.
- 普佛 ceremonies for general wellbeing.

### FAQ about online Buddhist prayer

**Q: Does the temple actually chant for my ancestor if I'm not there?**
A: Yes. This is a standard service in Chinese Buddhist temples. Most temples have set chanting hours during which the names of commissionable merits are included.

**Q: Will my online prayer actually make a difference?**
A: Within the Buddhist framework, yes — sincerity and form matter more than physical location. Whether you find this answer satisfactory depends on your understanding of how prayer works.

**Q: Is online prayer accepted by monks and temples?**
A: Yes — by every temple I work with. It's a normal service, not a back-up option.

**Q: How do I know the temple is legitimate?**
A: Look for: a real monastery (寺院) registration with the local Buddhist Association (佛教协会), evidence of a resident monastic community, and continuity of dharma lineage. A platform like fopusha.com works only with verified temples, but if you're working directly, ask to see the certification.

### The honest closing

I'm not here to argue the metaphysics. I'm here to say: distance from your hometown temple is a real problem, not a moral failing. There is a Buddhist answer to distance, and online services — done through legitimate temples — are part of that answer.

If you're an overseas Chinese wondering whether to commission something for a parent or grandparent: yes. The question is not whether it counts. The question is which temple, which ceremony, which timing. That's what fopusha.com helps with.

# Article 2: 牌位 (Ancestor Tablet): What It Means, What It Costs, and How Overseas Chinese Can Commission One

## 正文

牌位 (páiwèi). The character for tablet, 牌, is also the character in 牌照 (license plate) and 路牌 (street sign). The word 位 means "position" or "seat." Together, 牌位 is the seat or place carved for an ancestor in the temple.

I've written this article because in the 2 years since we launched fopusha.com, the most asked-about ritual among overseas Chinese is commissioning a 牌位. It's also the one with the most confusion about what it actually is.

Let me explain clearly, including the variants, the costs, and how overseas Chinese can actually do this.

### What a 牌位 physically is

In a Chinese Buddhist temple's 牌位堂 or 祖堂 (ancestor hall), you'll find rows of small wooden tablets, each about the size of a postcard, mounted on a wall or on shelves. Each tablet is engraved with the deceased's name, dates (often birth/death in the traditional Chinese calendar), and the name of the commissioning family.

The tablet itself is wooden, often with gold-leaf or red inscription. It sits at the temple for the term of the commission (1 year, 3 years, 99 years, or 永久 perpetual, depending on the temple and the tier).

What makes a 牌位 matter:

- In Buddhist practice, the 牌位 is the place where the ancestor's name is included in temple chanting rolls. When monks chant the 地藏经 (Earth Store Sutra) or 药师经 (Medicine Buddha Sutra), they chant from these rolls.
- In Chinese folk practice, the 牌位 is the spiritual "seat" through which ancestors receive the merit and offerings of the temple and the family.

Both readings are valid. Most Chinese Buddhists treat the 牌位 as both: a place in the temple community and a spiritual anchor for the ancestor.

### The three main types of 牌位

Through talking to many families and temples, here's the practical taxonomy:

**1. 永久牌位 (Permanent tablet)**

The most commonly commissioned option for overseas Chinese. The tablet is placed at the temple permanently. The ancestors' names stay on chanting rolls indefinitely.

Cost range in Chinese Buddhist temples: 5,000 to 50,000 RMB per tablet, depending on the temple's tier and the family's preferences.

Tier matters:
- A village temple might charge 1,000-3,000 RMB
- A county-level temple: 5,000-20,000 RMB
- A famous urban temple (e.g., named monastic centers): 30,000-100,000+ RMB

**2. 短期牌位 (Term tablet, 1-3 years)**

Some families want the tablet to be temporary. Most temples support 1-year, 3-year, or specific anniversary terms.

Useful for:
- Specific anniversaries or 49-day cycles
- Trial to see if the family wants long-term commitment
- A specific deceased ancestor whose situation is unclear

**3. 节庆牌位 (Festival tablet)**

For specific Buddhist festivals — Lunar New Year (春节), Ullambana (盂兰盆节), winter solstice (冬至), Qingming.

These are typically commissioned with annual renewal. Cost: 1,000-5,000 RMB per festival per tablet.

### Costs you should expect to budget

Beyond the actual tablet commission, here are the additional costs I tell overseas Chinese families to plan for:

1. **The tablet commission itself** (see above)
2. **Sutra chanting per cycle** — 300-3,000 RMB per sutra chant depending on length
3. **Lamp lighting (点灯)** — 200-1,000 RMB per lamp per year
4. **Plum flower offerings (供梅)** — Optional ancestral offering ceremonies, varies
5. **Annual renewal** — Most permanent tablets have a small annual renewal fee (typically 100-1,000 RMB)

For a comprehensive overseas Chinese family setup at a mid-tier temple:
- One permanent tablet: 10,000-20,000 RMB
- Annual sutra chants and lamp: 2,000-5,000 RMB
- Festival tablets (1-2 festivals/year): 2,000-10,000 RMB
- Annual total beyond first year: 5,000-15,000 RMB

Most families I work with budget around $1,500-$3,000 USD per year for ongoing Buddhist services for parents and grandparents.

### How to commission one as an overseas Chinese

There are three paths, listed by how I've seen them work:

**Path 1: Direct through a relative in China**

If you have a trusted family member in China, they can call the temple, visit the temple, and do all the paperwork in person. They pay locally. You fund them.

Pros: cheapest, most direct.
Cons: depends on family member's commitment and knowledge; pricing and process vary widely.

**Path 2: Through a temple's own overseas office**

Many major Chinese Buddhist temples (especially in Taiwan, Hong Kong, Singapore) have international offices that handle overseas Chinese commissions directly. Examples: Fo Guang Shan, Tzu Chi, City of Ten Thousand Buddhas.

Pros: official path with reputation.
Cons: English / Mandarin / Cantonese access varies; specific dates and ceremonies may not be flexible.

**Path 3: Through a platform like fopusha.com**

For overseas Chinese who don't have a relative in China and don't want to navigate direct temple administration, dedicated platforms coordinate the entire process — tablet commission, sutra chanting, festival scheduling, annual renewal.

Pros: English-first process, vetted temples, transparent pricing, package-based.
Cons: platform markup (typically 15-25%), less direct relationship with the temple.

### What to look for in a temple or platform

If you're commissioning a 牌位, here's what I'd verify:

- **Temple certification.** Real 寺院 have a 宗教活动场所登记证 (Religious Activity Site Registration Certificate), issued by the local religious affairs bureau.
- **Affiliation.** The temple should be a member of the official 佛教协会 (Buddhist Association) — there's one at national, provincial, and city levels.
- **Resident monastic community.** A real temple has monks who live there. Purely commercial "temples" without resident monastics exist; avoid them.
- **Transparent pricing.** The cost breakdown should be communicated in writing before commissioning.
- **Documentation.** You should receive a 牌位 commission record / 功德主 record.

If any of these are missing, walk away.

### FAQ about 牌位

**Q: What if my grandparent was not particularly Buddhist? Can I still commission a 牌位?**
A: Yes. The Buddhist tradition allows for ancestors regardless of their personal religious practice. The 牌位 is for the ancestor's spiritual wellbeing, not for the ancestor's prior belief.

**Q: How long does it take for the tablet to be commissioned?**
A: Typically 1-6 weeks from the paperwork being filed, depending on temple schedule and whether the tablet must be specially made.

**Q: Can I commission multiple tablets?**
A: Yes — most families commission for parents, grandparents, great-grandparents as a chain.

**Q: Are there any prohibited names or situations?**
A: Generally no — though temples may have rules on legal name precision (avoiding duplicates, ensuring the full name is engraved correctly).

**Q: Can I update the tablet info later?**
A: Most temples allow updates within a grace period. After engraving, corrections require a new tablet.

### One last thought

牌位 is not a magical solution. It's a Buddhist practice of remembering, dedicating, and integrating an ancestor within a temple community. For overseas Chinese separated from their ancestral home, the 牌位 is one of the most concrete paths to maintaining the connection.

If you're going to do this: verify the temple, document the commissioning, budget for the annual cycle, and let the practice become your own. It works whether you're in California or Guangdong, as long as you treat it sincerely.

# Article 4: Can Buddhist Practice Be Done Online? A Diaspora Perspective

**Target keyword**: can Buddhist practice be done online

## 正文

This is the question I'm asked most often, in almost every conversation about fopusha.com. The answer, as you'd expect from how the question is phrased, depends on which kind of practice you mean.

Let me try to give a thoughtful answer, drawing on tradition, what modern practice actually looks like for diaspora Chinese, and where the lines are.

### Why this is even a question

In a sense, this question only arises because physical distance is the modern condition of diaspora communities. For 2000+ years, Chinese Buddhism assumed physical presence — a temple, a community, an ordained practitioner.

The overseas Chinese diaspora — now estimated at 40+ million globally — has changed this assumption. Many diaspora Chinese live at a distance that makes physical participation in their family temple impossible. The question "can I do it online?" arises precisely because the alternative isn't "in person at a temple" but "not at all."

### What Buddhist practice means, in stages

For Chinese Buddhism, "practice" can mean different things. Let me list the layers:

**Daily practice (功课):**
Personal, daily, at home. Chanting the 心经 (Heart Sutra), 往生咒, taking refuge in the Three Jewels, meditation. None of this requires a temple.

**Group practice (共修):**
Chanting sutras together, dharma assemblies, group meditation. Many overseas Chinese sanghas do this via Zoom or WeChat groups. Online format works for these.

**Ritual practice (法会 / 仪式):**
Specific ceremonies led by monks (e.g., 盂兰盆 assembly, 三皈五戒 ceremony). Traditionally in-person; online format has emerged especially since 2020.

**Temple-located practice (寺院道场):**
Practices that happen at the temple — daily morning/evening chanting, special monastic ceremonies. The temple community is the host.

### Online works for some, less for others

| Practice | Online viable? | Notes |
|----------|----------------|-------|
| Daily personal chanting | Yes | At home alone, no platform needed |
| Group practice (Zoom / WeChat sangha) | Yes | Many diaspora sanghas do this regularly |
| Sutra recitation at temple | Yes | Commissionable service; monks chant at temple, you receive photos/recording |
| Memorial ceremonies for deceased | Yes | 49-day cycle, 周年 — standard remote services |
| Permanent tablet commissioning | Yes | Remote commissioning through temples' registration |
| Lighter ritual offerings (供灯, 上供) | Yes | Temple staff performs with name on record |
| Ordination ceremonies | Partially | Some ordinations can be virtual; most main traditions require in-person |
| Retreat / monastic training | No | Requires physical presence and extended stay |
| Monastic ordination (出家) | No | Requires monastic community and physical ordination |

The pattern: anything that involves commissioned service by a monastery for someone not physically present — yes, online. Anything that requires the practitioner to be there as part of the practice itself — depends on the lineage.

### What tradition says about location

Two perspectives from within Chinese Buddhist tradition:

**The strict position:**
Some monastics maintain that the ritual must be in-person — the form (仪轨) requires it, the lineage is transmitted through physical transmission, the merit deduction relies on the community of monastic presence.

This is a minority position in modern Chinese Buddhism. Most monks I work with have a more pragmatic view.

**The flexible position:**
The mainstream tradition holds that location is a secondary constraint. The primary requirements are (1) sincere intention, (2) correct form as transmitted through the lineage, (3) the merit arises through the action regardless of place.

The Vinaya Pitaka (the Buddhist monastic code) does not specify physical co-presence as a requirement for merit dedication. The Lotus Sutra and the Earth Store Sutra both have passages on universal reach of compassionate intent.

In practice, Chinese Buddhism has had centuries of "remote" practice — war, plague, and travel restrictions have all forced distance practice. The question of online is really an extension of an existing logic, not a novel invention.

### What "counting" might mean

Let me give a more philosophical answer. There are two ways to think about what makes a Buddhist practice effective.

**The first**: Buddhist practice works through spiritual mechanics that are empirically trackable. By this view, the question "does it count online?" is empirical — it could in principle be tested.

Buddhist traditionalism doesn't actually offer that empirical answer. It offers lineage transmission, textual reference, and community testimony — but not measurement.

**The second**: Buddhist practice works through focused intention and community participation in a virtuous act. By this view, "counting" has different criteria:
- Was the practitioner's intention sincere?
- Was the form correctly carried out?
- Was the dedication meaningful to the community (family / lineage / wider community)?
- Does the practice reinforce the practitioner's own path?

Under this view, online practice is "counting" because it accomplishes all four. The merit arises through the act itself, mediated by the temple's monastic chanting and the family's commissioned participation.

I personally find the second framing more accurate. And more useful for the diaspora community whose participation depends on exactly this distinction.

### The honest middle path

A few reasonable principles I'd suggest:

1. **Distance is a constraint, not a moral failing.** Most diaspora Chinese don't visit temples regularly because of distance. That's a fact, not a problem to solve.
2. **Online practice is legitimate when it involves real monastic communities.** A temple with monks actually chanting, with lineage, with documentation.
3. **Some practices require presence.** Ordination, monastic training, certain festivals of family tradition — these are in-person only.
4. **Sincerity matters more than form.** A performed ritual with sincere intention is more meaningful than a perfectly formal ritual done mechanically.
5. **Family lineage and community matter.** Your practice connects you to your family, your ancestors, and your lineage. Online doesn't break this — it operates through it.

### FAQ about online Buddhist practice

**Q: Is online practice legitimate Buddhist practice?**
A: Most Chinese Buddhist temples and monastics accept it as legitimate for commissioned services, group chanting, and personal practice. It is not a substitute for all forms of practice (e.g., ordination).

**Q: Can I teach my children Buddhist practice if it's online?**
A: Yes — many families do dharma classes and sutra recitation via WeChat or YouTube. Children can participate meaningfully.

**Q: Will my lineage be preserved through online practice?**
A: Yes — what carries lineage is the dharma transmission, the monastic community's continuity, and the family's ongoing commitment. Online vs in-person doesn't change these elements.

**Q: Are online sanghas real communities?**
A: Many are — they meet regularly, support each other, organize in-person retreats annually, maintain temple connections. Some are shallow. Choose quality.

### Closing thought

Buddhism has always adapted to its communities. The diaspora Chinese community is a real, large, and now multi-generational Buddhist community. Our practices have to adapt to the distance we live with. Online practice is one of those adaptations.

Not everywhere. Not for everything. But for many of the practices that matter most to overseas Chinese — ancestor veneration, memorial services, family practice, festival observance — online is the path that works. And it works because the underlying structure (sincere intention, correct form, real temple community) is intact.

If you're asking the question because you're hesitating: my honest answer is, start. Begin with one simple practice — one sutra at home, one service through a verified temple. See how it lands. That's how everyone begins.
